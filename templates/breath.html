The respiratory system
The respiratory system is represented by a number of anatomical entities. Classically, they are divided into airways (upper and lower) and respiratory organs. The upper airways are the nasal cavity, nasal and oral parts of the pharynx. The lower ones are the larynx, trachea, and bronchi. The respiratory organs include the lungs. In common parlance and in fact, when talking about the human respiratory organs, separate anatomical formations and respiratory tracts may be meant. For example, the larynx, the trachea are not only part of the lower respiratory tract, but also independent organs.
  • Air enters the lungs not by itself, it must be "sucked in", which occurs in the process of inhalation. This involves the diaphragm, the external intercostal and intercartilaginous muscles. During inhalation the diaphragm flattens somewhat, the thorax expands, which provides progressive movement of air into the airways and lungs.
  • Having passed all the way from nostrils to the final branches of bronchial tree, air gets into closed "bubbles" — alveoli. They are the main functional part of the lungs.
  • But how does oxygen reach its final destination — the organs? The outside of the alveolus is covered by a network of small blood vessels, through which blood flows continuously. One type of blood cell is the red blood cell, filled with the substance hemoglobin. It is this substance that carries the gases in the body.
  • Oxygen from the alveolar air "seeps" into the blood, where it is "captured" by hemoglobin. At the same time, carbon dioxide, a product of cellular activity, enters the alveolus. The oxygen-enriched red blood cells carry it around the body, and the carbon dioxide is released into the external environment by exhalation. Unlike breathing in — always an active process — exhalation is passive, but can also be active if necessary. Active exhalation also involves muscles — internal intercostal and abdominal wall muscles.
  • Negative intrapleural pressure plays a certain role in the breathing process.
  • The human breathing process is complex and regulated in various ways.
  • The respiratory center, a cluster of nerve cells in the medulla oblongata, is responsible for breathing.
  • The flow of nerve impulses goes to the muscles responsible for breathing, giving them a certain range of motion. The respiratory center has an automatism: about once every four seconds there is excitation, which stimulates the muscles that provide a breath. Then it is replaced by inhibition, the muscles of inhalation relax - there is an exhalation. Rhythmic change of these states - an innate property.
  • The frequency and depth of breathing depends on the intensity of oxidation processes occurring in the body. Physical activity leads to increased oxygen uptake and increased concentration of carbon dioxide in tissues and blood. The latter activates the respiratory center through the blood, and, as a consequence, the contraction of respiratory muscles is intensified. This allows faster removal of excess carbon dioxide and make up for the lack of oxygen.
Humans were formed in an environment with a certain amount of oxygen. For optimal breathing, however, it is not only the presence of oxygen, but also certain characteristics of the air we breathe in. These are provided by our respiratory tracts, so the lungs, normally, receive purified, humidified and warmed air.
