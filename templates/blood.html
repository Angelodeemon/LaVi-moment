The circulatory system
The circulatory system, also called the cardiovascular system, is a vital organ system that delivers necessary substances to all cells for essential functions. The cardiovascular system is a network consisting of the heart as a centralized pump, the blood vessels that distribute blood throughout the body, and the blood itself to transport various substances.

The circulatory system is divided into two distinct circles: the shorter pulmonary circle, which exchanges blood between the heart and lungs for oxygenation; and the longer systemic circle, which distributes blood to all other systems and tissues of the body. Both of these circles begin and end in the heart.
The primary function of the circulatory (or cardiovascular) system is to deliver oxygen to the tissues of the body and at the same time remove carbon dioxide produced by metabolism. Oxygen is bound to molecules called hemoglobin, which are found on the surface of red blood cells in the blood.

Starting from the heart, deoxygenated blood (containing carbon dioxide) returns from the systemic circulation to the right side of the heart. It is pumped into the pulmonary circulation and delivered to the lungs, where gas exchange occurs. Carbon dioxide is removed from the blood and replaced with oxygen. The blood is now oxygenated and returns to the left side of the heart.

From there it is pumped into the systemic bloodstream, delivers oxygen to the tissues, and returns to the right side of the heart. The blood also serves as an excellent transport medium for nutrients, such as electrolytes, as well as hormones. In addition, the blood transports waste products, which are filtered out of the blood in the liver.
