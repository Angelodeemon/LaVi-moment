The nervous system
The human nervous system is a complex of anatomically and functionally interconnected nervous structures that provide regulation and coordination of human body activity and its interaction with the environment. Nervous system functions integratively, linking sensitivity, motor activity and other regulatory systems (endocrine and immune) into a single whole; it also provides regulation of vital functions, interaction and adjustment of organism's activity to changing environmental conditions and events, carries out human mental activity as the basis of its social existence.
The human nervous system is often divided into central nervous system (CNS) and peripheral nervous system (PNS). The central one consists of the brain and spinal cord. The PNS consists of all other nerves and neurons that do not lie within the CNS. The predominant majority of the nerves belong to the PNS. The peripheral nervous system is divided into the somatic nervous system and the autonomic nervous system.
The somatic nervous system is responsible for coordinating body movement and receiving and transmitting external stimuli. This system regulates actions that are under conscious control.
The autonomic nervous system is divided into the parasympathetic and sympathetic nervous systems. The sympathetic nervous system is responsible for danger or stress and, among many physiological changes, can cause an increase in heart rate and blood pressure and excitement of the senses through an increase in adrenaline in the blood. The parasympathetic nervous system, on the other hand, is responsible for the resting state and provides pupil contraction, heart slowing, dilation of blood vessels and stimulation of the digestive and urinary systems.
